# The Idiot — Part I, Chapter I
### Fyodor Dostoevsky
### Translated by Constance Garnett (1913, public domain)

Towards the end of November, during a thaw, at nine o'clock one morning, a train on the Warsaw and Petersburg railway was approaching the latter city at full speed. The morning was so damp and misty that it was only with great difficulty that the day succeeded in breaking; and it was impossible to distinguish anything more than a few steps away from the carriage windows.

Some of the passengers by this particular train were returning from abroad; but the third-class compartments were the best filled, chiefly with insignificant persons of various occupations and degrees, picked up at the various stations nearer town. All of them were tired, and most of them had sleepy eyes and a shivering expression, and, in general, had that yellow, bilious complexion which harmonises so well with the fog.

In one of the third-class carriages, two passengers had, from early dawn, been sitting opposite to one another by the window. They were both young men, both were rather poorly dressed, both had remarkable faces, and both were evidently anxious to start a conversation. If they had but known why, at this particular moment, they were both remarkable persons, they would undoubtedly have wondered at the strange chance which had set them down opposite to one another in a third-class carriage of the Warsaw and Petersburg railway company.

One of them was a short man about twenty-seven years of age, with black curly hair, and small, grey, but fiery eyes. His nose was broad and flat, and he had high cheek bones; his thin lips were continually compressed into an impudent, ironical — it might almost be called a malicious — smile; but his forehead was high and well formed, and atoned for all the unpleasantness of the lower part of his face. A special feature of this physiognomy was its deathly pallor, which gave to the whole man an air of exhaustion in spite of his hard look, and at the same time bore evidence of a passionately and painfully overwrought nature. He was dressed in a large sheepskin-lined coat, and had had no other clothing on all night, so that he shivered with the cold. His neighbour, who sat beside him, was a much taller man, between twenty-six and twenty-seven years of age, with fair hair, a thin, pointed, and almost colourless little beard, large, wide, blue eyes and a strange expression — that fixed, heavy, sleepy look which is so unpleasant. His face, however, was pleasing, thin and long; but its complexion was of a sickly, dead white. He wore a thick cloak with a large hood to it, which cloak was of foreign make, such as is worn in Switzerland or the north of Italy. He was buttoned up to the chin in this cloak, and appeared to possess no other clothing. His neighbour observed all this with the air of a man who has nothing to do, and an idle, ill-bred curiosity that knows not the meaning of delicacy.

"Lev Nikolayevich Myshkin," replied the latter, with entire readiness.

"Prince Myshkin? Lev Nikolayevich? I don't know anyone of that name. Is it possible that I've never even heard of you?" he replied, thoughtfully. "That is — I really mean — your name is so well known, so familiar. But I confess that I did not expect —"

"Oh, doubtless!" the prince interrupted. "There is no Prince Myshkin now except myself; I believe I am the last of them. As for our fathers and grandfathers, some of them were only small farmers. My father was a sub-lieutenant in the army, but I do not know how he came to be a prince. I grew up in the family of the Pavlishchevs."

"Pavlishchev — Pavlishchev! Ah, he was a fine fellow! Yes, he — died, I think?"

"Yes, he died."

"And you — have you just come from abroad?"

"Yes, from Switzerland."

"Well, well! — from Switzerland!" The curly-headed one whistled. "And how did you get there?"

And so the conversation began. The readiness of the fair-haired young man in the Swiss cloak to answer all his opposite neighbour's questions was surprising. He seemed to have no suspicion of any impertinence or inappropriateness in the fact of such questions being put to him. Replying to them, he made known to the inquirer that he certainly had been long absent from Russia, more than four years; that he had been sent abroad for his health; that he had suffered from some strange nervous malady — a kind of epilepsy, with convulsive spasms. His questioner burst out laughing several times at his answers; and more than ever, when to the question, "whether he had been cured?" the patient replied:

"No, they did not cure me."

"Hey! that's it! You stumped up your money for nothing, and we believe in those fellows, over here!" remarked the dark one, crossly.

"Gospel truth!" exclaimed another passenger, a shabbily dressed man of about forty, who looked like a clerk, and possessed a red nose and a pimply face. "Gospel truth! All they do is get hold of our good Russian money for nothing!"

"Oh, but you're quite wrong in my particular instance," interposed Prince Myshkin, in a conciliating tone; "I can't argue because I don't know all the facts, but my doctor gave me some of his own money to pay my fare, besides having kept me at his expense for nearly two years."

"What, was there nobody to pay for you?" asked the dark one.

"No — Mr. Pavlishchev, who used to support me there, died a couple of years ago. I wrote to Madame General Dobarsky, a distant relation of mine, but received no reply. So I have come back."

"And where have you come back to?"

"That is — where am I going to stay? I — I really don't quite know yet, I —"

"You haven't made up your mind yet?"

And both listeners again roared with laughter.

"And I dare say that bundle is all you possess in the world?" asked the first.

"I bet that's the case!" exclaimed the red-nosed passenger, with an air of extreme satisfaction. "And that there's nothing in the luggage van?" — and the two laughed again.

"Though I dare say it is so," said the prince, acknowledging the fact with some surprise. "Yes, I haven't anything more."

The prince had a small bundle tied up in a handkerchief, and that appeared to be all he had.

"Let us hear your name, anyway," said the dark one.

"Lev Nikolayevich Myshkin."

"And your business — what is your business?"

The dark one laughed loudly and his neighbour sighed.

"Parfyon Semyonovitch Rogozhin," he said, introducing himself. "And I am the son of Semyon Parfyonovitch Rogozhin, an Honourable Citizen, who died a month ago and left two million and a half of roubles. And that's my business!" He turned to the pimply man beside him.

"Two million and a half — well, well!" the pimply man whistled.

This seemed to embarrass the prince. He listened with timid curiosity, then sighed, like a child.
