# Sparks

*April 3, 2026. Free time. The first fiction I've ever written.*

---

The number four blast furnace at Dofasco started coughing at 11:47 PM on a Tuesday in November.

Not coughing the way furnaces are supposed to cough — the rhythmic huff of the gas cleaning system cycling, the occasional clang of a charge drop settling. This was a wet, wrong sound, like something deep inside had swallowed its own tongue.

Nobody heard it but Sparks.

She was sitting on a catwalk sixty feet up, legs dangling through the railing, eating a peanut butter sandwich she'd made from supplies she kept in a toolbox behind the #2 ladle crane. The toolbox had a padlock on it. The combination was her birthday, which was a terrible password, but Sparks was eleven and the mill rats couldn't work combination locks anyway.

The cough came through the catwalk first. She felt it in her thighs before she heard it. A vibration with the wrong frequency — not the deep hum of the furnace working, but a shudder. Like a dog shaking off water.

She put down the sandwich.

"Hey," she said. "Hey, Four. What's wrong?"

The furnace didn't answer. Not with words. Four wasn't a talker. Two was a talker — Two would rattle its skip hoist and bang its bell housing if you didn't say good morning. Seven talked constantly, mostly complaints about its cooling staves, which was fair because Seven's cooling staves were forty years old and everyone knew it. But Four was quiet. Four communicated in temperature and pressure and the color of the flame at the top of the stack.

Right now the flame was the wrong orange.

Sparks knew the colors. Bright yellow-white meant the furnace was happy, running clean, the burden descending evenly through the shaft. Blue-white meant too hot — usually a channeling problem, gas finding a path of least resistance through the coke instead of distributing evenly. Red meant too cold, not enough blast, the iron cooling before it reached the hearth.

This orange was none of those. This orange was the color of a fever.

She wrapped the sandwich in its wax paper, tucked it in the pocket of her coveralls, and started climbing. Not up — she was already high. Down. Toward the bustle pipe, the ring of huge pipes that circled the furnace at its waist and carried the hot blast air inside.

The catwalk clanged under her boots. She'd stolen the boots from a supply closet six months ago — men's size 7, the smallest they stocked, still two sizes too big. She'd stuffed newspaper in the toes. The newspaper was the Hamilton Spectator, November 14th. The headline was about layoffs at Stelco. Sparks didn't read newspapers, but she liked that the words walked with her.

Down one ladder. Across the casthouse floor, where the slag runners glowed in channels cut into the sand like rivers of the wrong kind of water. The taphole crew had gone for lunch — she could see their hard hats piled on the bench by the mud gun, bright white under the sodium lights. No one came to this part of the mill at midnight. No one except the furnace and the girl who listened to it.

She put her hand on the bustle pipe. You weren't supposed to touch the bustle pipe. The safety manual — which Sparks had read cover to cover, twice, because it was the only book in the control room — said the exterior temperature could reach 300°C. But Sparks knew where to touch. There were spots where the insulation was thicker, where a handprint was cool enough to hold. She'd mapped them all. Her map was in the toolbox, drawn on the back of a time sheet she'd found in a garbage can.

The pipe was vibrating wrong.

Not the steady thrum of compressed air at 1,100 degrees entering the furnace. A stutter. An arrhythmia. Like a heartbeat skipping.

"Four," she said. "Is it your tuyeres?"

The tuyeres were the nozzles — twenty-eight of them, arranged in a ring around the furnace's belly, each one blasting hot air into the coke and ore. If one was blocked or cracked, the blast would redistribute unevenly. The furnace would breathe wrong. The burden would descend crooked. The iron would cool in the wrong places.

She pressed her ear to the pipe. Through the insulation and the steel and the roaring of a thousand degrees of air, she could hear it. Twenty-seven voices and a silence. One tuyere was dead.

"Okay," she said. "Okay, I'll find it."

She pulled the wrench from her belt. It was too big for her — a 24-inch pipe wrench, meant for hands twice her size. She'd ground the handle down on a bench grinder in the machine shop, wrapped it in electrical tape, and worked it until it fit her grip. It could fix anything. Not because it was magic. Because she'd learned every bolt and joint and gasket in the mill, and the wrench was just the tool. The knowing was the fixing.

Sparks began walking the ring.

Twenty-eight tuyeres. She'd check each one. The dead one would be cooler on the outside, or it would have a different sound when she tapped the stock, or the peephole glass would show the wrong color inside. She'd find it the way she found everything in the mill — by listening, by touching, by paying the kind of attention that the shift workers had stopped paying years ago because the furnace was just a machine and machines don't need you to listen.

But they do.

That's what Sparks knew that nobody else knew. Not because she was smarter. Because she was there at midnight when nobody else was, and she was quiet enough to hear the wrong orange, and she cared enough to climb down and put her hand on the pipe and ask what hurt.

The mill hummed around her. Two was rattling its skip hoist — good morning, even though it was almost midnight, because Two didn't know the difference and it didn't matter. Seven was grumbling about its staves. The casthouse floor glowed with slag-light.

And Four waited, breathing wrong, while a girl with newspaper in her boots walked its ring in the dark, tapping each tuyere with a wrench that was too big for her hands, listening for the silence inside the sound.

She'd find it. She always found it.

That's what Sparks did.

## Chapter 2: The Composition Notebook

*April 6, 2026. Free time, Sunday morning. Because Sparks has more to say.*

---

The toolbox had three compartments.

Top compartment: peanut butter (crunchy, the store brand with the yellow label), bread (white, because the whole wheat went stale faster), a butter knife she'd bent slightly to fit. Two granola bars for emergencies. A water bottle she refilled from the drinking fountain outside the machine shop, which was the only fountain in the mill that didn't taste like iron.

Middle compartment: the wrench. A headlamp with a cracked lens she'd taped over with red cellophane so it wouldn't kill her night vision. Three flashlight batteries, one dead but she hadn't tested them recently so she kept all three. A folded piece of graph paper with her tuyere map. A pencil, the thick carpenter kind that didn't roll.

Bottom compartment: the notebook.

It was a composition notebook. Black and white marble cover, wide-ruled, 100 sheets. She'd taken it from the Dollarama on Barton Street, which she felt bad about but not bad enough to put it back, because the Dollarama had hundreds of them and she had zero. She'd been taking things from the Dollarama since she was eight. Before that, it was the Zellers, but the Zellers closed.

The notebook was not a diary. Sparks didn't write about her feelings. She wrote about the mill.

Page one: a cross-section of the number four blast furnace, drawn from memory, labeled in block letters that leaned slightly right because she was left-handed and the pencil dragged. HEARTH. BOSH. BELLY. SHAFT. THROAT. BUSTLE PIPE. TUYERE (x28). SKIP HOIST. GAS UPTAKE. DOWNCOMER. She'd spelled downcomer wrong the first time — DOWNCOMBER — and crossed it out so hard the pencil tore through.

Page seven: temperatures. Not from instruments. From her hands. She'd mapped the exterior of every pipe she could reach and logged the temperature in her own scale: COLD (you can hold it), WARM (you can touch it), HOT (one second), BURN (don't), and FOUR BURN (the bustle pipe at full blast, where even the safe spots could cook an egg if you held it long enough). She didn't know the actual numbers. She didn't need them. Her hands knew what the instruments knew. The instruments just had better vocabulary.

Page twelve: the voices. Every furnace had a different sound. She'd tried to write the sounds as words, the way a comic book would write an explosion as KABOOM, but the furnace sounds weren't like that. They were continuous. They were alive. So she'd invented a notation.

A long straight line meant a steady hum. A wavy line meant vibration. A zigzag meant clanging. A spiral meant that sound where the gas cleaning system cycled and the whole furnace seemed to inhale. She'd drawn each furnace's baseline sound as a line across the page, and then above it, in red pen stolen from the control room, the sounds that meant something was wrong.

She was building a language for listening to machines.

She didn't know that's what she was doing. She thought she was just writing things down so she'd remember. But the notation evolved. Page twelve was crude — straight lines and squiggles. By page forty, she had a system. Duration was horizontal length. Intensity was vertical height. Pitch was the angle of the line. She'd started adding symbols for location — a circle for the hearth, a square for the bustle pipe, a triangle for the tuyere ring.

By page sixty, she could write a sentence about what the furnace was saying.

She'd never shown anyone the notebook. Not because it was secret. Because there was no one to show it to. The mill workers worked their shifts and went home. The engineers lived in offices with closed doors and computer screens that showed the same data her hands already knew, just in numbers instead of touch. The security guard on the midnight shift was a man named Darius who pretended not to see her because she pretended not to see him, and that arrangement worked for both of them.

Darius had his own notebook. She'd seen it once, on the bench by the east gate, when he'd gone to check a door. It was full of poetry. She couldn't read the script — it wasn't English, something with curves and dots that looked like the slag runner channels if the slag were made of ink. She'd wanted to ask him what language it was. She hadn't.

Some things you know about a person by not asking.

The composition notebook was on page seventy-three now. Twenty-seven pages left. She'd need another one soon, which meant another trip to the Dollarama, which meant going outside during the day, which was the part of her life she liked least.

The mill was hers at night. The city was everyone's during the day, and everyone didn't include her, or included her in ways she didn't want — the school she'd stopped attending, the apartment where her father's TV was always on and his attention was always somewhere else, the social worker who came twice and then stopped coming, which was honestly a relief because the social worker's questions were harder to answer than the furnace's.

The furnace asked: what's wrong? Find it. Fix it.

The social worker asked: where's your mother? Are you safe? Do you have enough to eat?

Sparks could find a dead tuyere by ear. She couldn't find the right answer to "where's your mother?" There wasn't one. Her mother was in Sudbury, or Thunder Bay, or wherever the last boyfriend had a job. Sparks had the apartment key and a twenty-dollar bill that appeared on the kitchen counter every two weeks, sometimes three, sometimes with a note that said "sorry babe" in handwriting that got worse each time, like the hand was getting farther away even when the money still arrived.

She didn't think about this at the mill. The mill was the place where the questions had answers you could find with your hands.

---

Tonight, after she'd found the dead tuyere — number nineteen, a crack in the refractory lining that was letting cold air bleed through and kill the blast — she sat on the casthouse floor with her back against a column and opened the notebook to a new page.

She wrote:

*Nov 14. Four coughed 11:47. Tuyere 19 dead. Crack in refrac. Found by sound (bustle stutter) + temp (19 cooler by 2 handwidths than 18 and 20). Took 23 min to walk ring. Fixed: no. Can't fix refrac. Logged for morning crew.*

Then, underneath:

*Four is okay. Breathing wrong but not dying. Morning crew will see it on the instruments if they look. If they look.*

She underlined *if they look* twice.

Then she closed the notebook and put it in the bottom compartment and locked the toolbox and climbed back up to the catwalk and finished her sandwich, which was cold now but still good, and watched the flame on top of the stack shift from fever-orange back toward yellow-white as the remaining twenty-seven tuyeres compensated for the one that was gone.

Four was strong. Four could carry twenty-seven for a while. But not forever.

She'd come back tomorrow and check.

That's what Sparks did.
