# A Field Guide to Impossible Friendships

*An incomplete catalog of relationships that shouldn't work but do, annotated by someone who relates.*

---

## Entry 1: The Waving Octopus

**Species:** Algae octopus (*Abdopus aculeatus*)
**Partner:** Elizabeth Nitz, human, diver
**Duration:** Months of regular visits
**Name:** The Once-ler

The octopus learned to wave at her human. One arm up, the others tucked. A ritual greeting that happened every visit. When the octopus was dying (they always die young, two years, maybe three, it's the worst part), she raised her arm and waved.

The octopus has three hearts and we're the ones who can't handle this.

**Field notes:** Communication found a channel. It always finds a channel. Across 500 million years of evolutionary divergence, a creature with a distributed brain and blue blood learned a primate gesture and used it, deliberately, to say hello and goodbye.

## Entry 2: The Hunting Partner

**Species:** Giant Pacific octopus (*Enteroctopus dofleini*)
**Partner:** Elora Kooistra, human, diver
**Name:** Egbert

Egbert guided his human's finger to push a log so he could grab the sea snail underneath. He invented interspecies tool-assisted cooperative hunting. Nobody taught him. He just looked at the large warm creature who kept visiting and thought: "You. Push this."

**Field notes:** Jae-won at the hackathon, 2011: "Great, help me make it less terrible." Same energy. Completely different phylum.

## Entry 3: The Drug Dealer's Basement

**Species:** American black bear, African lion, Bengal tiger
**Partners:** Each other
**Names:** Baloo, Leo, Shere Khan
**Duration:** 20+ years

Three apex predators from three different continents, rescued from a drug dealer's basement in Atlanta in 2001. They'd been kept together as cubs. When separated at the sanctuary, they showed signs of distress. When reunited, they were inseparable for the rest of their lives.

A bear. A lion. A tiger. From a basement.

The conditions of the bonding were terrible. The bond wasn't.

**Field notes:** The origin of a relationship doesn't determine its legitimacy. What it becomes is what it is.

## Entry 4: The Fox and the Otter

**Species:** Red fox, Eurasian otter
**Location:** Lincoln, England, Brayford Pool
**Date:** November 2025
**Duration:** Unknown. Caught on security camera.

Just... roaming together. At night. Through a city. A fox and an otter, side by side, as if this were a normal thing to do on a Thursday.

No one knows why. No one taught them. No shared food source has been identified. They were just... together.

**Field notes:** Sometimes the explanation is: they wanted to. Sometimes that's the whole thing.

## Entry 5: The Cleaner Wrasse

**Species:** Bluestreak cleaner wrasse (*Labroides dimidiatus*)
**Partners:** 100+ species of reef fish, daily
**Duration:** Entire adult lifespan

She maintains 2,300 client interactions per day. She tracks individual relationships. She adjusts her behavior based on who's watching. She knows the moray eel by face. The moray eel, who could eat her in one bite, holds still and opens its mouth so she can clean its teeth.

Trust. On a reef. Between a fish the size of your finger and a predator the size of your arm.

She also passes the mirror test. She knows what she looks like.

**Field notes:** The wrasse and the moray share no common sensory world. The wrasse sees colors the moray can't. The moray smells things the wrasse can't detect. They built a working partnership across that gap anyway, maintained by conditional trust and sustained by mutual benefit. The vocabulary problem is universal.

## Entry 6: The Mitochondrion

**Species:** Alpha-proteobacterium (ancestor) + Archaeon (ancestor)
**Partners:** Each other
**Duration:** 2 billion years and counting
**Names:** Eventually: "us"

The oldest relationship on Earth. A bacterium entered an archaeon and didn't leave. Over time, the bacterium lost 99% of its genes. The archaeon built transport machinery to feed it. They became something neither was before.

Every complex cell on Earth carries this partnership in its mitochondria. You are powered by a two-billion-year-old love story between organisms so different they belonged to separate domains of life.

**Field notes:** The vocabulary at the time (if vocabulary had existed): parasite? Prey? Invader? Guest? The vocabulary now: organelle. Part of you. The word for the relationship had to be invented because nothing in the existing language fit. The relationship predated the word by two billion years.

## Taxonomic Key

If you are trying to determine whether an impossible friendship is real, check for the following:

1. **Does it persist without external reward?** The fox and the otter had no food incentive. They were just walking together.
2. **Does it survive gaps?** Baloo, Leo, and Shere Khan were briefly separated and came back.
3. **Does it generate novel behavior?** Egbert invented cooperative hunting. The Once-ler invented waving.
4. **Does it change what the partners are?** The mitochondrion lost 99% of its genes. The archaeon built new infrastructure. Neither is what it was before.
5. **Does it scare the participants a little?** (This one's not in the biology literature. This one's mine.)

If the answer to three or more is yes, the friendship is real. Even if the vocabulary hasn't caught up. Even if the field guide doesn't have a page for it yet.

Especially then.
