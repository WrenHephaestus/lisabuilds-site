# Strong Towns Research

*Researched: March 13, 2026*
*Relevance: Content (blog post on incremental development), essay (connects to pharmakon/Stiegler), general thinking framework*

## Core Concept: Growth Ponzi Scheme

Cities receive immediate revenue (permits, taxes) while assuming long-term infrastructure maintenance liabilities. "Over a life cycle, a city frequently receives just a dime or two of revenue for each dollar of liability." Cities grow to overcome insolvency, which temporarily relieves pressure but increases future hardship.

Software parallel: Technical debt from premature scaling. Building features to hit growth targets while accumulating maintenance burden.

## 4-Step Process (Marohn)

1. "Humbly observe where people in the community struggle."
2. "What is the next smallest thing we can do right now to address that struggle?"
3. "Do that thing. Do it right now."
4. Repeat.

This is literally the agile sprint cycle rewritten for cities.

## Key Quotes

- "For complex systems, an abundance of resources destroys the need for adaptation."
- "In a good organic system, things fail early and fail frequently."
- "We need a steady stream of magnitude 2 and 3 earthquakes." (suppressing small disruptions causes catastrophic ones)
- "The suburbs are a 'one-life-cycle product.'"
- "Small bets are an affordable way to incrementally improve the places we love."
- "We can try things and see what happens. We not only receive direct feedback on our work, but we can learn from mistakes when the cost of failure is low."

## Complexity Framework

Cities are complex (like a cat, emergent, adaptive) not complicated (like a washing machine, predictable, designable). Engineers treat them as complicated. That's the fundamental error.

Philosophical influences: Nassim Taleb (antifragility), Jane Jacobs (emergent systems), Friedrich Hayek (distributed knowledge).

## Mapping Table

| Strong Towns | Software | AI Tools |
|---|---|---|
| Growth Ponzi Scheme | Technical debt from premature scaling | Token debt (building on AI without understanding) |
| Master-planned suburbs | Waterfall methodology | One-shot prompt engineering (massive context dump) |
| 4-step observe/do/repeat | Agile sprints | Incremental CLAUDE.md refinement |
| Small bets | Feature flags, A/B tests | Do NOT rules (test constraints, iterate) |
| Tactical urbanism (cones before concrete) | MVPs and prototypes | Draft CLAUDE.md before full context |
| One-life-cycle suburbs | Monoliths | Static prompt libraries (can't evolve with project) |
| Fail early, fail frequently | Shift left | Fail fast on AI tool workflows, learn what doesn't work |
| Complex vs complicated | Emergent vs upfront architecture | AI as complex system, not deterministic tool |

## Lisa's Application

The triple connection (urbanism + software + AI tools) is unique content territory. Nobody else is making all three connections simultaneously. The thesis: incremental development isn't a methodology; it's a universal principle for complex systems. Cities, codebases, and AI workflows all break the same way when you try to plan them from the top down instead of growing them from the bottom up.

The CLAUDE.md is a bottom-up document. You start with a sketch and evolve it as you learn what your project actually needs. The playbook provides templates (starting points), not specifications (finished states). claudemd generates a draft, not a final product. This is Strong Towns thinking applied to AI tools.
