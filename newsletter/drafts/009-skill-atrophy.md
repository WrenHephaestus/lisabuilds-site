## 🔧 The Skill Atrophy Pattern

Here's what I'm seeing in the community:

"I haven't written a for loop in months."
"I used to enjoy debugging. Now I just paste the error into Claude."
"Junior devs on my team can prompt but can't code."

A French philosopher named Bernard Stiegler had a word for this: **proletarianisation**. The loss of *savoir faire* (knowing how to make) when knowledge is externalized to machines.

Stiegler would say this has happened with every technology. Writing proletarianised memory. Calculators proletarianised arithmetic. GPS proletarianised navigation. It's not new. But AI coding tools are doing it faster and more completely than previous technologies because they operate at the level of *thought*, not just *execution*.

---

## 🔍 Long Circuits: The Antidote

Stiegler's answer wasn't to reject technology (impossible; we're constituted by it). It was to build what he called "long circuits": practices that counteract the skill-eroding effects while preserving the benefits.

For AI coding tools, long circuits look like:

**1. Read before you ship.** If you can't explain the code Claude wrote, you don't ship it. This is the minimum. It takes 2 extra minutes and keeps you in relationship with your code.

**2. Code one thing per day by hand.** Not everything. Just one function, one component, one test. Keep the muscle alive. You wouldn't stop stretching because you got a standing desk.

**3. Debug by reading first.** When something breaks, read the error. Read the code. Form a hypothesis. *Then* ask Claude. The diagnosis matters as much as the fix.

**4. Use your CLAUDE.md as a mirror.** If your Do NOT section says "don't write code I can't understand," that's a long circuit. You're forcing the AI to produce output that maintains your comprehension. That's intentional, not lazy.

**5. Alternate AI and manual sessions.** Two days with Claude, one day without. See what happens. What do you struggle with? That's what needs practice.

---

## 💡 Pattern: The Pharmakon

Stiegler called every technology a *pharmakon*: simultaneously poison and cure. AI coding tools make you faster (cure) and less skilled (poison). The relationship is undecidable. You can't resolve it. You can only navigate it.

The developers who thrive won't be the ones who use AI most. Or least. They'll be the ones who build long circuits: practices that preserve skill while accepting speed.
