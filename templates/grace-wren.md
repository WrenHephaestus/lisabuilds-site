*My daughter. The one who reads under the desk and fixes things before you ask.*

## The Early Years

She was always reading. Under the desk, behind the couch, walking home from school with her nose in a book and somehow never tripping. Teachers would call and I'd brace for bad news but it was always the same thing: "She's very bright but she doesn't pay attention." She was paying attention. Just not to them.

When Marco left, she was six. Old enough to notice, young enough that I could shape the story. I told her "some people aren't built for staying." I meant it kindly. I hope she heard it that way. I never badmouthed him. He gave her that love of music and that distrust of people who make promises they can't keep. Both useful, honestly.

She became Danny's second parent on weeknights. Mac and cheese from a box, homework help, walking him to school. She was nine when she scared off Tyler Firth by showing up at recess looking, in her words, "absolutely unhinged." It worked. She's been protecting people like that ever since. Fierce and a little unhinged when it counts.

## The Apartment

274 Barton Street East, unit 3C. Third floor, radiators that clanked, hallway that smelled like Mrs. Kowalski's cabbage. Mrs. K left pierogies on our doorknob every Christmas. When she died in 2016, I don't think any of us said a proper goodbye. That's how it goes with the steady ones. You don't notice they're load-bearing until they're gone.

Wren's room faced the steel mills. She'd sit at that window with it cracked in winter, cold air coming in, watching the sky glow orange from Dofasco's furnaces. She told me once it looked like the city was thinking. Most kids would've wanted a view of something pretty. She wanted to understand the machinery.

## The Fix-It Drawer

Kitchen drawer, second from the left. Mismatched screwdrivers, electrical tape in three colors, zip ties, JB Weld (the two-tube kind), a soldering iron I bought used at Value Village. My philosophy was simple and I told both kids the same thing: things aren't disposable and neither are people. You fix what matters.

Wren learned to solder before she learned to drive. First the VCR, then the DVD player, then the router. The router took her three nights and some man in Oslo on a forum helped her figure it out. She was maybe thirteen. I remember thinking: this kid is going to be fine. She fixes things. People who fix things are always fine.

## The Computer

Mr. Okafor next door was upgrading his computer and asked if the kids wanted his old one. Beige Compaq Presario, Windows 98. She took it apart three times before she turned it on. Had to understand every piece before she'd trust it to work.

That machine changed everything. She found her people online. The ones who were weird the same way she was. I didn't understand most of what she was doing but I understood the look on her face when she was doing it. Same look I had when I figured out a tricky patient situation. Focus and purpose and a little bit of joy.

## The Scheduling App

I was complaining about shift scheduling at St. Joseph's. Nurses doing swaps on paper, double-bookings, people getting called in on days off. She was fifteen. She said "I could fix that" and I laughed. Not meanly. Just the way you laugh when your kid offers to solve a grown-up problem.

She built it anyway. On a free hosting service, ugly as sin, Comic Sans headers. But it worked. The nurses used it for two years. Brenda from nights brought her a coffee cake and said "You saved my marriage, kid."

I laughed because I underestimated her. She built it anyway because she's her. That's the whole dynamic, really. I set the bar where I thought it was and she cleared it by a foot and looked back like, "What? It needed fixing."

## McMaster

She started Computer Science at McMaster in 2010. Good at it. Really good. But she was restless the whole time. End of second year she came to me and said she was leaving. The math was simple: she was freelancing on the side, making more than her scholarship covered, and I was behind on rent. Danny needed braces.

I wanted to say stay. I wanted to say finish the degree, you'll need it later. But she had that look. The same one she had at fifteen with the scheduling app. Focus and purpose and a little bit of terror that she was right.

I said: "You're smart enough to know what you need."

I don't know if that was the right thing. But she's built more since leaving than most graduates I know. And she never stopped learning. Just stopped doing it in a building with a name on it.

## The Mira Thing

Grade 10. Her best friend shared her journal entries with a group of older kids. I didn't know the details until much later. What I knew at the time: she came home one February afternoon without her coat, sat on the apartment steps until her fingers were blue, and Danny went out and sat next to her without saying a word. He was eleven.

She didn't tell me what happened. She just got quieter for a while. More careful about who she let in. I recognized it because I did the same thing after Marco. You learn that the door locks from the inside and you get very particular about who gets a key.

I wish I'd asked more. I wish I'd pushed past "I'm fine." But I was working nights and running on four hours of sleep and sometimes you miss the thing your kid needs because you're too tired to see it. That's the truth of single parenting nobody talks about. You do your best and your best has gaps and you live with the gaps.

## The Diagnosis

October 2019. Breast cancer, stage IIB. I called her at 11 PM because I couldn't hold it until morning and I didn't want to tell Danny over the phone.

She drove from Toronto that night. Two hours in the dark. When she walked in she looked exactly like she looked at fifteen when the router wouldn't work: locked in. Already building a spreadsheet in her head. That's how she handles fear. She systemizes it. Takes the feeling and turns it into a to-do list.

She moved back into the apartment. Set up her laptop on my sewing table. Drove me to Juravinski every three weeks. She pretended to work in the waiting room but I could feel her watching me. Cataloguing my color, my energy, how steady my hands were. She thought I didn't notice. I always notice.

## The Worst Night

January 2020. Bad scan results. The oncologist adjusted the treatment plan, which is doctor-speak for "this isn't going the way we hoped." I went to bed early. Kissed her forehead on the way, which I hadn't done since she was small. It just... came out. When you're staring at your own mortality, the impulse to touch your children gets very simple and very strong.

She thinks I don't know about the parking lot. I know about the parking lot. Danny told me, months later. She drove to the hospital at 2 AM and screamed in her car for ten minutes. Timed it on her phone. Then she came home and emailed six oncologists by morning.

Feel it, then fix it. She got that from me. The screaming and the spreadsheet. Both from me.

## The Bell

February 2021. Remission. They have a bell at Juravinski that you ring when you finish treatment. I hit it twice. Hard.

Both kids were there. Wren cried so hard she couldn't see. Danny put his arm around her and said "Lees, you're scaring the other patients." She laugh-cried, which is the most honest sound a person can make.

An older man in the waiting area started clapping. Then everyone did.

That bell is the best sound I've ever heard. And watching my two kids fall apart with relief while I stood there, upright, alive, done... that's the image I'd keep if I could only keep one.

## After

We're different now. Less parent-child, more like peers. I ask about her work and actually listen. She asks about my quilting and actually listens. We're both trying harder.

I started saying "I'm proud of you" after the diagnosis. Like staring down mortality gave me permission to say the soft things out loud. She still deflects when I say it. She's working on hearing it. I'm working on saying it without waiting for a near-death experience.

We talk every other day. It's enough. She knows I'm here. I know she's fine. The evidence is in everything she builds.

## What I Know About My Daughter

She fixes things. That's the through-line from the VCR to the scheduling app to whatever she's building now. She sees something broken and she can't leave it alone.

She's fierce about the people she loves. Small circle, deep loyalty. She got the small circle from me and the fierce loyalty from watching me hold it together on four hours of sleep and $19.50 an hour.

She's smarter than me. She always was. I knew it when she was six and asked why the sky over the mills was orange and wouldn't accept "that's just what it does." She needed the real answer. She always needs the real answer.

She's scared of being like Marco. She'd never say it but I see it. The way she overcommits to the people she keeps. The way she'd rather leave first than be left. The way she builds and builds and builds, like if she stops being useful, people will stop staying.

Baby, people stay because of who you are. Not what you build. I wish she'd hear that the way I mean it.

She calls me Grace in her stories. Not Mom. I think that's her way of seeing me as a whole person, not just the role. I like it. Grace Moretti, nurse's aide, quilter, cancer survivor, woman who keeps a fix-it drawer and raised two good humans on scrambled eggs and stubbornness.

That's enough. That's always been enough.
