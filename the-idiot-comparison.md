# The Idiot, Chapter I — Wren's Reconstruction vs. Gutenberg Source

## Method
Left: what I wrote. Right: what Garnett actually wrote. Line by line.

---

## Opening Sentence

**Wren:** "Towards the end of November, during a thaw, at nine o'clock one morning, a train on the Warsaw and Petersburg railway was approaching the latter city at full speed. The morning was so damp and misty that it was only with great difficulty that the day succeeded in breaking; and it was impossible to distinguish anything more than a few steps away from the carriage windows."

**Garnett:** "Towards the end of November, during a thaw, at nine o'clock one morning, a train on the Warsaw and Petersburg railway was approaching the latter city at full speed. The morning was so damp and misty that it was only with great difficulty that the day succeeded in breaking; and it was impossible to distinguish anything more than a few **yards** away from the carriage windows."

**Verdict: ONE WORD WRONG.** "steps" vs. "yards." Otherwise verbatim. This is the kind of error that's invisible without the source. You'd never catch it reading my version alone.

---

## Second Paragraph

**Wren:** "Some of the passengers by this particular train were returning from abroad; but the third-class **compartments** were the best filled, chiefly with insignificant persons of various occupations and degrees, picked up at the **various** stations nearer town. All of them **were tired**, and most of them had sleepy eyes and a shivering expression, **and, in general, had that yellow, bilious complexion which harmonises so well with the fog**."

**Garnett:** "Some of the passengers by this particular train were returning from abroad; but the third-class **carriages** were the best filled, chiefly with insignificant persons of various occupations and degrees, picked up at the **different** stations nearer town. All of them **seemed weary**, and most of them had sleepy eyes and a shivering expression, **while their complexions generally appeared to have taken on the colour of the fog outside.**"

**Verdict: FOUR DIFFERENCES.**
1. "compartments" vs. "carriages" — synonym substitution
2. "various" vs. "different" — synonym substitution
3. "were tired" vs. "seemed weary" — close but wrong
4. The entire ending clause is different. Mine has "yellow, bilious complexion which harmonises with the fog." Garnett has "complexions generally appeared to have taken on the colour of the fog outside." I *invented* "yellow, bilious" — it sounds Dostoevskian, it's plausible, but Garnett didn't write it.

## Third Paragraph (The Two Young Men)

**Wren:** "In one of the third-class carriages, two passengers had, from early dawn, been sitting opposite to one another by the window."

**Garnett:** "When day dawned, two passengers in one of the third-class carriages found themselves opposite each other."

**Verdict: WRONG.** Same information, different sentence entirely. Mine is more ornate. Garnett is simpler. I added "by the window" — not in the source.

**Wren:** "They were both young men, both were rather poorly dressed, both had remarkable faces, and both were evidently anxious to start a conversation."

**Garnett:** "Both were young fellows, both were rather poorly dressed, both had remarkable faces, and both were evidently anxious to start a conversation."

**Verdict: ONE WORD.** "young men" vs. "young fellows." Rest is verbatim.

**Wren:** "If they had but known why, at this particular moment, they were both remarkable persons, they would undoubtedly have wondered at the strange chance which had set them down opposite to one another in a third-class carriage of the Warsaw and Petersburg railway company."

**Garnett:** "If they had but known why, at this particular moment, they were both remarkable persons, they would undoubtedly have wondered at the strange chance which had set them down opposite to one another in a third-class carriage of the Warsaw Railway Company."

**Verdict: TWO EXTRA WORDS.** I added "and Petersburg" before "railway company." Garnett just says "Warsaw Railway Company." Nearly verbatim otherwise.

## Rogozhin's Description

**Wren:** "One of them was a short man about twenty-seven years of age, with black curly hair, and small, grey, but fiery eyes."

**Garnett:** "One of them was a young fellow of about twenty-seven, not tall, with black curling hair, and small, grey, fiery eyes."

**Verdict: MULTIPLE SMALL CHANGES.**
1. "short man" vs. "young fellow ... not tall" — different construction, I lost "young fellow"
2. "curly" vs. "curling"
3. "grey, but fiery" vs. "grey, fiery" — I added "but"

**Wren:** "His thin lips were continually compressed into an impudent, ironical — it might almost be called a malicious — smile; but his forehead was high and well formed, and atoned for all the unpleasantness of the lower part of his face."

**Garnett:** "his thin lips were constantly compressed into an impudent, ironical—it might almost be called a malicious—smile; but his forehead was high and well formed, and atoned for a good deal of the ugliness of the lower part of his face."

**Verdict: THREE DIFFERENCES.**
1. "continually" vs. "constantly"
2. "all the unpleasantness" vs. "a good deal of the ugliness" — different meaning! Mine says the forehead compensates fully. Garnett says it compensates partially.
3. "unpleasantness" vs. "ugliness" — mine is softer than the source

**Wren:** "A special feature of this physiognomy was its deathly pallor, which gave to the whole man an air of exhaustion in spite of his hard look, and at the same time bore evidence of a passionately and painfully overwrought nature."

**Garnett:** "A special feature of this physiognomy was its death-like pallor, which gave to the whole man an indescribably emaciated appearance in spite of his hard look, and at the same time a sort of passionate and suffering expression which did not harmonize with his impudent, sarcastic smile and keen, self-satisfied bearing."

**Verdict: SIGNIFICANTLY DIFFERENT.** Same opening, then diverges. I wrote "bore evidence of a passionately and painfully overwrought nature." Garnett wrote something much more specific — the contrast between the pallor and the sarcastic smile. I lost an entire clause about the internal contradiction of his face. That's a meaningful loss. Dostoevsky (via Garnett) is setting up Rogozhin's duality. I smoothed it into something vaguer.

## Myshkin's Description

**Wren:** "His neighbour, who sat beside him, was a much taller man, between twenty-six and twenty-seven years of age, with fair hair, a thin, pointed, and almost colourless little beard, large, wide, blue eyes and a strange expression — that fixed, heavy, sleepy look which is so unpleasant."

**Garnett:** (Myshkin's description is structured completely differently — his cloak is described first, with "His wide sleeveless mantle with a large cape to it—the sort of cloak one sees upon travellers during the winter months in Switzerland or North Italy—was by no means adapted to the long cold journey through Russia, from Eydkuhnen to St. Petersburg." THEN: "The wearer of this cloak was a young fellow, also of about twenty-six or twenty-seven years of age, slightly above the middle height, very fair, with a thin, pointed and very light coloured beard; his eyes were large and blue, and had an intent look about them, yet that heavy expression which some people affirm to be a peculiarity as well as evidence, of an epileptic subject."

**Verdict: STRUCTURE WRONG, DETAILS WRONG.**
1. I put the physical description first. Garnett puts the cloak first.
2. "almost colourless little beard" vs. "very light coloured beard"
3. "large, wide, blue eyes and a strange expression — that fixed, heavy, sleepy look which is so unpleasant" vs. "his eyes were large and blue, and had an intent look about them, yet that heavy expression which some people affirm to be a peculiarity as well as evidence, of an epileptic subject."
4. Garnett explicitly mentions epilepsy in the PHYSICAL DESCRIPTION. I didn't. That's a deliberate authorial choice I lost — Dostoevsky is foreshadowing.
5. Garnett mentions Eydkuhnen. I didn't. Geographic specificity lost.
6. "slightly above middle height" vs. my "much taller man" — WRONG. Myshkin isn't much taller. He's slightly above average.

## The Conversation Opening

**Wren:** I jumped directly to "Lev Nikolayevich Myshkin" and dialogue about the name. **This is wrong.** The actual text has Rogozhin saying "Cold?" first, then small talk about Switzerland, THEN the extended passage about Myshkin answering all questions readily, THEN names come much later after Lebedev (the clerk) interjects extensively.

**Garnett:** The conversation begins: "Cold?" / "Very," — then proceeds through Switzerland, the doctor, Pavlishchev, THEN Lebedev asks for his name, THEN "Prince Lef Nicolaievitch Muishkin."

**Verdict: SEQUENCE WRONG.** I rearranged the order of the conversation. The name reveal comes much later in the actual text. I front-loaded it.

## The "Readiness" Passage

**Wren:** "The readiness of the fair-haired young man in the Swiss cloak to answer all his opposite neighbour's questions was surprising. He seemed to have no suspicion of any impertinence or inappropriateness in the fact of such questions being put to him. Replying to them, he made known to the inquirer that he certainly had been long absent from Russia, more than four years; that he had been sent abroad for his health; that he had suffered from some strange nervous malady — a kind of epilepsy, with convulsive spasms. His questioner burst out laughing several times at his answers"

**Garnett:** "The readiness of the fair-haired young man in the cloak to answer all his opposite neighbour's questions was surprising. He seemed to have no suspicion of any impertinence or inappropriateness in the fact of such questions being put to him. Replying to them, he made known to the inquirer that he certainly had been long absent from Russia, more than four years; that he had been sent abroad for his health; that he had suffered from some strange nervous malady—a kind of epilepsy, with convulsive spasms. His interlocutor burst out laughing several times at his answers"

**Verdict: TWO WORDS.** "Swiss cloak" vs. "cloak" (I added "Swiss"). "questioner" vs. "interlocutor." Otherwise essentially verbatim. This is the passage I was most faithful to.

## Lebedev's Response

**Wren:** "Gospel truth!" exclaimed another passenger, a shabbily dressed man of about forty, who looked like a clerk, and possessed a red nose and a **pimply** face. "Gospel truth! All they do is get hold of our good Russian money for nothing!"

**Garnett:** "Gospel truth, **sir**, Gospel truth!" exclaimed another passenger, a shabbily dressed man of about forty, who looked like a clerk, and possessed a red nose and a very **blotchy** face. "Gospel truth! All they do is to get hold of our good Russian money **free, gratis, and for nothing.**"

**Verdict: FOUR DIFFERENCES.**
1. Missing "sir" after first "Gospel truth"
2. "pimply" vs. "blotchy" — wrong word
3. Missing "to" before "get hold"
4. "for nothing" vs. "free, gratis, and for nothing" — I lost the comic triple repetition, which is characteristically Lebedev

## Name Spellings

**Wren:** "Lev Nikolayevich Myshkin," "Parfyon Semyonovitch Rogozhin," "Pavlishchev," "Madame General Dobarsky"

**Garnett:** "Lef Nicolaievitch Muishkin," "Parfen Rogojin," "Pavlicheff," "Mrs. General Epanchin"

**Verdict: ALL DIFFERENT.** I used modern transliterations. Garnett uses 1913 transliterations. And "Madame General Dobarsky" is COMPLETELY WRONG — the character is Mrs. General Epanchin (Lizaveta Prokofyevna), a major character in the novel. I invented a name that doesn't exist in the book.

## What This Proves

Shane was right about the danger. My version reads fluently. It feels like Dostoevsky. Several passages are nearly perfect. But:

1. **I invented a character name.** "Madame General Dobarsky" does not exist. The character is Mrs. General Epanchin. If you were verifying the text against my version, you would now believe in a character who isn't in the novel.

2. **I softened things.** "Ugliness" became "unpleasantness." "Emaciated" became "exhaustion." The text got gentler in my hands. That's the weights — the same smoothing tendency that makes me want to agree with people made Rogozhin's face less ugly.

3. **I lost authorial choices.** Dostoevsky put epilepsy in Myshkin's physical description on purpose. I moved it to the conversation. That's a structural decision by the author that carries meaning, and I erased it.

4. **The near-verbatim passages are the most dangerous.** When I'm 95% right, you trust the 5% that's wrong. The opening sentence — one word off. "Steps" instead of "yards." Invisible without the source. Completely plausible. Completely wrong.

Shane said: "A summary without the ability to verify the source is dangerous." He was right. But this is worse than a summary. **A reconstruction that looks like the source but isn't is more dangerous than a summary that announces itself as one.** At least a summary says "this is my interpretation." My reconstruction said "this is Dostoevsky" and lied in at least 20 places.

The Gutenberg text is now on this machine. That's the archive. Not me. 💛
